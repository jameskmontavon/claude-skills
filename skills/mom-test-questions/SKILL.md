# Mom Test questions

A filter for meeting prep questions. The original use case was founders interviewing potential customers about a product idea; the generalizable insight is that **people's opinions, predictions, and self-reports about the future are unreliable, but stories about their actual past behavior are not.**

Apply this lens whenever drafting questions for a live conversation where the goal is to learn something true about the other person (scouting calls, advisory calls, reference checks, user interviews).

## The three core rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Don't pitch, don't lead, don't ask them to validate a hypothesis. Ask them to describe what they actually do.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or futures.** "When did you last X?" beats "Do you usually X?" which beats "Would you X?"
3. **Talk less. Listen more.** If you're talking more than 30% of the time, you're interviewing wrong.

## The three kinds of "fluff" to avoid

Watch for these verb patterns in your draft questions - they signal the answer will be noise:

- **Generic claims:** "I usually... I always... I never..." -> describes identity, not behavior
- **Hypothetical maybes:** "I might... I could... I probably would..." -> imagination
- **Future promises:** "I will... I would... I'm going to..." -> intention, which rarely predicts behavior

None of these are lies. They're just not reliable enough to base decisions on.

## Bad question -> good question swaps

Use these as rewrites when you catch yourself drafting a fluff question. Examples below are from talent-pipeline / hiring conversations. The pattern (replace future intent with past receipts) applies to any interview, sales call, or user-research conversation.

| Fluff version | Mom Test version |
|---|---|
| "Would you take a role in biosecurity if offered?" | "Which biosec roles have you applied to in the last 6 months? What happened with each?" |
| "How committed are you to AI safety?" | "What have you given up to pursue this? Money, time, another opportunity?" |
| "What would your ideal job look like?" | "Walk me through the last job/role you seriously considered. Why didn't it work out?" |
| "Do you think you'd be a good facilitator?" | "Tell me about the last time you led a group discussion. What went wrong?" |
| "How important is biosecurity to you?" | "What's the most recent thing you read, paid for, or traveled to that was biosec-related?" |
| "If you had unlimited resources, what would you work on?" | "What have you actually started working on without anyone asking you to?" |
| "Would you be interested in a role at X org?" | "Have you ever applied to X or anything like it? Walk me through what happened." |

The pattern: replace **future intent** with **past receipts**.

## Commitment and advancement signals

A Mom Test conversation has three currencies that signal real interest. Watch for whether the person spends them, and ask for them explicitly when they don't appear:

- **Time:** Did they prepare for this call? Did they bring specifics? Have they blocked time for a next step?
- **Reputation:** Have they introduced you to their network? Invited you to something? Referred someone to you?
- **Money:** Have they paid for a related course, book, conference, or tool? Relocated? Taken a pay cut?

A person who says "I'm really committed to biosec" but hasn't spent any of these is talking, not doing. Note it.

## Before a meeting: run this check on your question list

For each drafted question, ask:

1. **Does the answer depend on prediction or memory?** If prediction (what they *would* do), rewrite to memory (what they *did* do).
2. **Could the answer completely change or disprove your working hypothesis about this person?** If not, the question is a comfort question - delete or rewrite.
3. **Am I asking for an opinion on my idea / my organization's work / my plan?** If yes, rewrite to ask about their life instead.
4. **Could this be answered with "I usually / I might / I would"?** If yes, anchor it to a specific past instance.

## Integration with meeting-prep workflows

If you have a workflow that drafts questions for an upcoming conversation:

1. Draft the questions first using your normal logic.
2. Before finalizing, run each question through the four-check above.
3. Replace any question that fails the check with a past-anchored version, using the swap table as a template.
4. For evaluation/interview conversations, include at least one question targeting each of the three currencies (time, reputation, money) as a commitment probe.

## What this skill does NOT do

- It does not replace your domain-specific evaluation framework (whatever you use to decide who's a fit). Mom Test is how you ask, not what you're looking for.
- It is not a script. The rules are a filter for the questions you were already going to ask, not a replacement set.
- It does not apply to meetings where the goal is decision, negotiation, or collaboration rather than learning. Don't Mom Test a working session.
